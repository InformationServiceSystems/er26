The company operates as a specialty food distributor, sourcing products from suppliers around the world and selling them to a network of business customers. Each customer is a company identified by a short code and characterized by its company name, a primary contact person with a title, a mailing address including city, region, postal code, and country, and telephone and fax numbers. A customer may place multiple orders over time, though some customers who have been set up in the system may not have placed any orders yet. The business needs to maintain a complete record of every customer regardless of their order history, since even dormant accounts may become active again.

When a customer places an order, the order is recorded with the date it was placed, the date by which the customer expects delivery, and a freight charge representing the shipping cost. The freight charge should never be negative. Each order is assigned to a specific employee who is responsible for processing it, and every order must have both a customer and a responsible employee associated with it at the time it is created. An order consists of one or more line items, and an order with no line items would not be meaningful. Each line item specifies a particular product, the quantity being ordered, the unit price agreed upon for that transaction, and an optional discount expressed as a fraction between zero and one. The quantity on each line item is expected to be a whole number greater than zero, and the unit price should not be negative. It is possible for different line items on the same order to reference different products, and the same product may appear across many orders placed by different customers.

The product catalog organizes items into categories such as Beverages, Condiments, and Confections. Each category has a name and a brief description of the kinds of products it contains. Every product belongs to exactly one category, and a category may contain many products. Products are tracked by name, list price, the number of units currently in stock, the number of units on order from the supplier, a reorder level, and a flag indicating whether the product has been discontinued. When the stock on hand for a product drops below its reorder level, the product is considered to need reordering from the supplier. A product that has been marked as discontinued should not be added to any new orders, although historical orders that included the product before discontinuation remain valid and are kept on record. The business relies on this distinction to ensure that staff processing new orders do not inadvertently select items that are no longer available.

Each product is sourced from exactly one supplier. A supplier is a company with a contact person, address details similar to those kept for customers, telephone and fax numbers, and optionally a reference to the supplier's home page. A supplier may provide multiple products, and the relationship between a product and its supplier is expected to remain stable once established. The company depends on timely communication with suppliers, particularly when reorder levels are triggered, so having accurate and up-to-date contact information for each supplier is considered essential.

The company employs a team of staff members, each identified by a unique number and described by their first and last name, job title, date of birth, hire date, home address, home telephone number, an internal telephone extension, and free-text notes summarizing their background. Employees are organized in a reporting hierarchy: most employees report to a single manager, who is also an employee. However, the top-level executive does not report to anyone, and it is not possible for an employee to report to themselves. This hierarchy is used for approval workflows and performance tracking. Employees are also responsible for handling customer orders, and a given employee may handle many orders over time, while each order is handled by exactly one employee.

For sales territory management, the company divides its market into broad geographic regions such as Eastern, Western, Northern, and Southern. Each region is identified by a number and a descriptive name. Within each region there are one or more territories, each with its own identifier and description. Every territory falls within exactly one region. Employees are assigned to territories to define their sales coverage area, and this assignment is flexible: a single employee may serve multiple territories, and a single territory may be covered by more than one employee. The territories assigned to an employee collectively determine which geographic area that employee is responsible for, and this information is used for sales reporting and workload distribution.

Once an order is ready to be shipped, it is assigned to a shipping company. The company works with several shippers, each identified by a number and characterized by a company name and telephone number. A shipped order records the date it was dispatched and the shipper that handled the delivery. However, not every order has been shipped at any given point in time. Orders that are still being processed or are awaiting shipment will not have a shipper or a shipped date assigned to them. A shipper may deliver many orders, and the choice of shipper for a particular order may depend on factors such as destination, cost, and delivery speed.

Taken together, the system should ensure that every order is tied to a known customer and a responsible employee, that each line item points to a recognized product with sensible quantity and pricing information, that products are properly categorized and linked to their suppliers, that the employee hierarchy is maintained without circular reporting, and that territory assignments provide clear coverage of all sales regions. The business expects that discontinued products are kept out of new transactions, that reorder alerts are raised when stock levels fall below thresholds, and that shipping information is recorded accurately once an order leaves the warehouse.
